My first real dilution exercise involved preparing a 1:100 dilution of a protein stock solution to run a Bradford assay. I had done the math on paper, the numbers looked right, and I was confident. Then our instructor ran the actual absorbance readings — and mine were off by nearly an order of magnitude.

What happened? I had confused the **dilution factor** with the **volume ratio**. A 1:100 dilution means 1 part solute in 100 parts total volume — not 1 part solute plus 100 parts solvent. That single misreading threw off my entire standard curve.

## The C1V1 = C2V2 Formula

The dilution formula is deceptively simple:

```
C1 × V1 = C2 × V2
```

Where:
- **C1** = initial concentration
- **V1** = volume you need to take from the stock
- **C2** = desired concentration
- **V2** = final volume of the diluted solution

For my 1:100 dilution of a 10 mg/mL stock into a final 1 mL volume:

```
10 mg/mL × V1 = 0.1 mg/mL × 1 mL
V1 = 0.01 mL = 10 µL
```

So I needed 10 µL of stock topped up to 1 mL with buffer. Simple — except I had added 100 µL instead of 10 µL because I conflated "1:100" with "add 100 µL."

## Serial Dilutions: Where Error Compounds

Serial dilutions amplify small mistakes. If you make a 1:10 dilution error in step 1, every subsequent step inherits that error multiplicatively. A three-step 1:10 serial dilution has a theoretical final dilution of 1:1000 — but if step 1 is slightly off, so is 1:100 and 1:1000.

The two most common mistakes I've observed (and made):

1. **Not mixing between steps.** Each tube in a serial dilution must be vortexed or pipetted up and down to homogenize the solution before taking the aliquot for the next step. Skipping this creates a concentration gradient within the tube.

2. **Reusing the same pipette tip.** If you aspirate from the previous tube with a contaminated tip, you carry over concentrated solution and flatten your dilution curve.

## A Simple Checklist That Helped Me

Before any dilution experiment, I now go through this:

- [ ] Write out the C1V1 = C2V2 calculation on paper or in my notebook
- [ ] Label all tubes *before* pipetting (not during or after)
- [ ] Confirm which volume is my aliquot vs. my diluent
- [ ] Use a fresh tip for each transfer
- [ ] Vortex or mix each tube for at least 5 seconds before the next step
- [ ] Verify my total final volume makes sense (sanity check)

## The Bigger Lesson

The real takeaway was not about the formula — it was about slowing down. Lab errors rarely happen because someone doesn't know the concept. They happen because of rushing, autopilot mode, or skipping the "obvious" steps.

Since that first mistake, I keep a small paper notebook dedicated to calculations. Every dilution gets written down, checked, and signed off before I touch a pipette. It feels slow at first. It saves time in the long run.

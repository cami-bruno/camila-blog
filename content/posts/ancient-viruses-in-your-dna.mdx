I want to tell you something about yourself that will either fascinate you or mildly freak you out.

Probably both.

About 8% of your DNA doesn't come from your human ancestors. It comes from viruses. Ancient retroviruses that infected predecessors of our species millions of years ago, integrated their genetic material directly into the genome, and have been passed down through every generation since — sitting inside every cell of every person alive today.

When I first properly understood this, I had to sit with it for a minute.

## How does a virus end up in your DNA forever?

Most viruses infect a cell, use it to replicate, and leave. The virus and the cell go their separate ways, usually with the cell losing. But a specific type — retroviruses — work differently.

Retroviruses carry RNA as their genetic material. When they infect a cell, they reverse-transcribe their RNA into DNA and insert it directly into the host cell's genome. HIV is a retrovirus. That's why it's so difficult to fully eliminate — it becomes part of your DNA.

Usually, this is still temporary. The retrovirus infects a body cell, replicates, and that insertion doesn't get passed to offspring.

But occasionally — rarely — a retrovirus infects a germline cell. A sperm cell or an egg. And when that happens, the viral DNA becomes permanent. It passes to every cell of every offspring, and their offspring, across millions of years.

These ancient integrated sequences are called endogenous retroviruses, or ERVs. And they make up about 8% of our genome.

## They're not just junk

For a long time ERVs were dismissed as "junk DNA" — fossil remnants that did nothing, passive passengers drifting through evolutionary time. Most of them are, in fact, degraded beyond recognition. Millions of years of mutations have broken them down into fragments that just sit there.

But some of them have been repurposed. Evolution does that — it takes whatever's available and finds a use for it.

One of the most striking examples: a protein called syncytin, which is essential for forming the placenta in mammals. Without it, the placenta can't fuse properly, and the pregnancy can't be maintained.

Syncytin originally comes from a retrovirus. An ancient infection, integrated into the genome, and then repurposed by evolution into something essential for mammalian reproduction. We carry a viral protein that helps make pregnancy possible.

There are other examples — ERV sequences that appear to be involved in immune regulation, in embryonic development, in gene expression. The more researchers look, the more they find that this "junk" isn't entirely junk.

## What this does to my brain

Both reactions happened for me — the freaked out one and the amazed one. And I couldn't really separate them.

Freaked out because there is viral DNA inside every one of my cells right now, as there has been since before I was born. Viral sequences that are as much a part of my genome as anything else about me.

Amazed because we're walking archives. Every human carries millions of years of evolutionary history embedded in their DNA — including the record of infections that our ancestors survived. And some of those scars became features.

The virus that infected some small primate ancestor 100 million years ago is still here, in a sense, in you. That's not a small thing.

## The part that makes me want to keep reading

There's still so much we don't understand about ERVs. New functions are being discovered regularly. The "junk DNA" framing has been almost entirely abandoned in the scientific community at this point — too much of it turns out to be doing something.

We carry the history of life's encounters with viruses inside us. And we're still figuring out what that history means.

That's the kind of thing that keeps me reading papers I only half understand at midnight. Totally worth it.

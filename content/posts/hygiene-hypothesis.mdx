Every time the seasons change, my body has an opinion about it. My nose disagrees with spring. I've just accepted this as a fact of life — that's just how I am.

But is it? Because the more I read about the hygiene hypothesis, the more I wonder if "just how I am" is actually a product of the environment I grew up in. And whether that environment was partly shaped by companies that profit from me being afraid of germs.

## What the hygiene hypothesis actually says

In the late 1980s, an epidemiologist named David Strachan noticed something: children who grew up in larger families, especially with older siblings, had significantly lower rates of allergies and hay fever. His explanation was counterintuitive — more siblings meant more infections, more shared germs, more microbial exposure. And that exposure, he argued, was protecting them.

The idea is this: the immune system needs to learn. In early life, it's searching for things to respond to, trying to calibrate itself. If it grows up in an overly sterile environment with very little microbial contact, it doesn't develop properly. It stays on high alert with nothing real to fight — and starts overreacting to harmless things like pollen, pet dander, or dust.

Allergies, in this framing, aren't just bad luck. They might be a symptom of a system that never got the exposure it needed to calibrate correctly.

## The numbers back this up

Rates of allergies, asthma, and autoimmune diseases have risen sharply in industrialized countries over the past 50 years. In rural areas and less industrialized parts of the world, these conditions are significantly less common.

Kids who grow up on farms have lower rates of allergies. Children who attend daycare early — more contact, more germs — show lower rates too. Studies on gut microbiome diversity consistently show that people with more diverse gut bacteria tend to have fewer immune-related conditions.

This doesn't mean dirt cures allergies. The hypothesis is more nuanced than that, and there's still active debate about the exact mechanisms. But the general direction of evidence is consistent: our immune systems evolved alongside microbes. They expect them. When they don't get them, something goes wrong.

## The part that bothers me most

Here's what I can't stop thinking about.

There's an entire industry built on convincing you that germs are the enemy. Antibacterial soaps, antibacterial sprays, products that promise to eliminate 99.9% of bacteria on every surface in your home. The marketing is everywhere and it has been incredibly effective at making people afraid of microbial contact.

But when you kill everything — the harmful AND the beneficial — you're not protecting your immune system. You might be depriving it of exactly what it needs.

And the companies selling these products know this. They have research departments. They understand microbiology. But there's no profit in telling you to let your kids play outside, stop sterilizing everything, and think twice before buying their antibacterial wipes.

I'm not saying hygiene doesn't matter — it absolutely does. But there's a difference between basic hygiene and the obsessive sterility that's been marketed to modern consumers as a necessity. That difference is worth questioning, especially when the people blurring it are the ones selling you the solution.

## What I take from this

I probably can't reverse whatever in my upbringing left me with seasonal allergies. But thinking about this has made me genuinely more skeptical of health claims that come packaged with something to sell.

The immune system isn't an enemy of microbes. It grew up with them. It learned from them. Maybe our relationship with bacteria needs to be less about elimination and more about understanding what we actually need — and who benefits when we don't.

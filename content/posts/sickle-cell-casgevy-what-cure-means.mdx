Imagine waking up at 3am with pain so sudden and total that you can't locate where it's coming from — it's just *everywhere*. Your bones. Your chest. Your abdomen. The kind of pain that sends you to the emergency room, where you'll spend the next few days on opioids waiting for it to pass. Then it does. And you go home knowing it'll come back. Maybe next month. Maybe next week. Maybe tomorrow.

That's a vaso-occlusive crisis. And for hundreds of thousands of people with sickle cell disease, it's not a hypothetical — it's Tuesday.

I've been thinking about this disease a lot lately, because in December 2023, a therapy was approved that headlines called a "cure." And something about that word bothered me. Not because the therapy isn't remarkable — it genuinely is. But because I think we owe it to the people living with this disease to be precise about what we mean.

So let's be precise.

---

## One mutation. One amino acid. One lifetime.

Sickle cell disease comes from a point mutation — a single nucleotide change in the *HBB* gene, the gene that encodes the beta-globin subunit of hemoglobin. Specifically: adenine becomes thymine at position 17 of the coding sequence. That swap changes one amino acid: glutamic acid (hydrophilic, negatively charged) becomes valine (hydrophobic, nonpolar) at position 6 of the beta-globin protein.

One amino acid out of 147. One change out of 3 billion base pairs in the human genome.

And it's enough to collapse an entire physiology.

Normal hemoglobin molecules slide past each other easily inside red blood cells, which stay round and flexible — able to squeeze through capillaries narrower than themselves. But the mutant hemoglobin (called HbS) behaves differently: when oxygen levels drop — which happens constantly, in every tissue, every second — HbS molecules stick to each other and polymerize into rigid fibers. The cell distorts into a crescent, a sickle shape. It becomes stiff. It gets stuck.

Stuck cells block blood vessels. Blocked vessels starve tissues of oxygen. Starved tissues send pain signals. That's the vaso-occlusive crisis. Do that hundreds of times over a lifetime and organs — kidneys, spleen, brain — accumulate damage they never fully recover from.

It's an inherited disease, autosomal recessive: you need two copies of the mutant allele to have the full condition. One copy gives you sickle cell *trait* — usually not symptomatic, but important for genetic counseling, which I'll come back to.

About **300,000 babies are born with sickle cell disease every year**. It's not rare. It's neglected.

## The elegant workaround: what Casgevy actually does

Here's the part that genuinely fascinates me — and the part most headlines get wrong.

Casgevy (exagamglogene autotemcel, developed by Vertex Pharmaceuticals and CRISPR Therapeutics) does **not** fix the HBB mutation. The broken gene is still there in every cell. What Casgevy does is something more conceptually interesting: it exploits a biological switch that already exists in your body.

The key is **fetal hemoglobin (HbF)**.

Before birth, fetuses produce a different form of hemoglobin — HbF — which has higher oxygen affinity (useful for extracting oxygen from the mother's blood across the placenta). HbF does not sickle. In people with sickle cell disease, HbF would be essentially curative — if they kept making it.

The problem is that shortly after birth, a gene called **BCL11A** activates and functions as a transcriptional repressor: it turns HbF production off and switches the body to adult hemoglobin (HbA — or in the case of sickle cell patients, HbS). This switch is permanent under normal circumstances.

What CRISPR does in Casgevy: it edits BCL11A in hematopoietic stem cells — the blood-forming stem cells that live in bone marrow — specifically disabling the erythroid-specific enhancer region that drives BCL11A expression in red blood cell precursors. Less BCL11A → the repression on HbF is lifted → cells start making fetal hemoglobin again.

HbF dilutes HbS below the threshold needed for polymerization. No polymerization → no sickling → no crises.

**The procedure:**
1. Doctors extract the patient's own hematopoietic stem cells
2. The cells are edited ex vivo (outside the body) using CRISPR-Cas9
3. The patient undergoes myeloablative chemotherapy — essentially clearing the bone marrow to make room for the edited cells. This part is genuinely hard: weeks of hospitalization, serious side effects
4. Edited cells are infused back. They engraft, multiply, and produce HbF-making red blood cells — ideally for life

The clinical results are striking. In the pivotal trial, **29 out of 30 patients** with sufficient follow-up were free from vaso-occlusive crises for at least 12 consecutive months after treatment. The longest follow-up is now approaching five years, with stable HbF levels and sustained editing. The data, so far, holds.

## So: is it a cure?

This is the question I keep coming back to. And I think the honest answer is: *it depends on what you mean by cure.*

In oncology, "cure" usually means eradication — no cancer cells detectable, sustained over time. The cause is gone.

With Casgevy, the cause — the HBB mutation — is still in every cell. What's gone is the manifestation: the sickling, the crises, the organ damage. The body has been rerouted around the broken road.

A published analysis in *Medicine, Health Care and Philosophy* makes this point carefully: gene therapy "cure" is being used to describe at least three different outcomes — normalization of the body, obtaining a normal life, and a change in identity. These are not the same thing. And patients and physicians may not be attaching the same meaning to the word.

That matters practically. If a patient believes they're cured in the absolute sense, they might stop monitoring, stop communicating symptoms, stop coming in for follow-up. If the HbF levels ever dropped — theoretically, years later — they'd need to know to act on that.

What I'd call Casgevy: a **functional remission with high durability potential**. That's less catchy than "cure." It's also more honest.

## $2.2 million. Now let's talk about that.

Casgevy is priced at approximately **$2.2 million USD per patient** in the United States.

I want to sit with that number for a second, because I think it's easy to read it and move on.

Two point two million dollars. For one patient. One treatment.

My first reaction when I learned the price was the same as yours probably is: that can't be what it actually *costs* to make. And you'd be right — it isn't. The manufacturing and administration costs are real but a fraction of that number. The rest is:

**R&D debt recovery.** Developing a drug from early research to FDA approval takes 10–15 years and costs, on average, $1–3 billion — and that estimate already includes the cost of all the drugs that failed along the way. Every failed Phase 2 trial, every candidate that didn't make it, every year of research that produced nothing — the company paid for all of that. None of it will ever generate revenue. So the drugs that do make it carry the debt of the ones that didn't.

**The orphan drug premium.** Sickle cell disease in the US affects roughly 100,000 people. Of those, far fewer qualify for Casgevy. When you sell to a small population, you charge more per unit — there's no other way to recover costs at low volume. The US Orphan Drug Act actually incentivizes this by giving exclusivity and tax breaks to companies developing rare-disease drugs, because without those incentives, no one would invest in the first place.

**Investor returns.** Vertex is a publicly traded company. CRISPR Therapeutics is a publicly traded company. Their investors expect returns commensurate with the risk they took funding years of research before a single dollar of revenue. That expectation is baked into the price.

**"Value-based" pricing.** Vertex's stated rationale: managing severe sickle cell disease over a lifetime costs $4–6 million in hospitalizations and care. A one-time fix that eliminates that burden is "worth" $2.2M. This logic is not irrational. It is, however, contingent on someone having $2.2M or insurance that covers it.

In the US, most patients don't pay this directly — private insurers and Medicaid negotiate and pay. But uptake is still extremely slow: roughly 40 patients were treated in all of 2024, Casgevy's first full year on the market. 111 in 2025. After two years of availability, we're talking about approximately 150 people worldwide who have received it.

Now: where do most sickle cell patients live?

**About 80% of cases are in Sub-Saharan Africa.** Countries where $2.2M is not a number that maps onto any existing healthcare framework. As of early 2026, as Uganda rolls out mandatory nationwide newborn screening for sickle cell disease — identifying more patients than ever — the gene therapy remains completely inaccessible. Vertex has not announced differential pricing for low- and middle-income countries.

## The part that stays with me

A substantial portion of the foundational science that made Casgevy possible — Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier's CRISPR work (Nobel Prize, 2020), Feng Zhang's work on Cas9, decades of hemoglobin biology — was funded by public money. NIH grants. University research budgets. Taxpayer dollars.

The science was built collectively. The price was set privately.

I'm not saying Vertex is evil. Clinical development genuinely costs billions. The regulatory work is real. The manufacturing is complex. The risk was real. Someone had to fund it.

But there is a genuine structural tension here that I think our field has a responsibility to reckon with: the patients who most need transformative gene therapies are, almost by definition, the ones least able to access a system that prices them as premium goods.

The science of sickle cell disease is, in a meaningful sense, solved. We know the mutation. We know the mechanism. We have a therapy that works. The problem is no longer biological.

That's either the most hopeful sentence in this post, or the most frustrating one — depending on how you look at it.

I think it's both.

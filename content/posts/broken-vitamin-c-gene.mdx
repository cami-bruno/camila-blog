I have a gene that doesn't work. You do too. Every human alive has a broken version of a gene called GULO sitting in their DNA, and it's been broken for about 61 million years.

GULO, when functional, produces an enzyme that synthesizes vitamin C. Most mammals make their own — dogs, cats, cows, rats, horses all carry a working version and never once need to think about scurvy. We can't do that. And the fossil of the gene we used to use is still right there in our genome, nonfunctional, just sitting.

I find this genuinely difficult to fully wrap my head around.

## What happened

About 61 million years ago, an ancestor of primates accumulated mutations in the GULO gene. The mutations broke it. The enzyme stopped working. That individual, and all their descendants, could no longer produce vitamin C internally and had to get it entirely from food.

Normally, losing a function like that would be catastrophic. But here's the thing: at that point in evolutionary history, our primate ancestors were living in tropical environments eating a diet rich in fruit. Vitamin C was everywhere. The ability to synthesize it internally wasn't providing any survival advantage — the food was already supplying plenty of it.

When a trait confers no advantage, there's no evolutionary pressure to maintain it. Mutations accumulate in unused genes. Eventually the gene stops working. And since vitamin C was freely available in the diet anyway, nothing bad happened. The organism survived, reproduced, passed on the broken gene.

Over millions of years, the broken gene became fixed in the entire population. Every primate inherited it. Including us.

## The pseudogene just sits there

What I find most striking is that the gene didn't disappear. It's still in our genome, recognizable, occupying the same chromosomal position it always did. It's called a pseudogene now — a sequence that looks like a gene, has the structure of a gene, but doesn't produce a functional protein.

It's a molecular fossil. Evidence of a function that worked for hundreds of millions of years, then became unnecessary, then broke down — preserved in the genome like a record of something that used to matter.

And GULO isn't alone. The human genome contains an estimated 15,000 pseudogenes. Remnants of genes that worked in our ancestors. Some might have functions we haven't discovered yet. Most are probably just there.

## The part that keeps me thinking

I genuinely believe that in biology, things tend to be the way they are for a reason. Not in a designed sense — I don't mean that — but in the sense that systems this complex, shaped across millions of years of selection, rarely carry things around with no purpose at all.

But pseudogenes challenge that. Here's a gene that demonstrably doesn't do its original job. It's broken. The reason it's still here isn't function — it's simply that evolution doesn't clean up after itself. There's no mechanism to actively remove nonfunctional sequences that aren't causing harm. They persist.

*Unless* they're not entirely functionless.

Some pseudogenes have been found to produce small RNA molecules that regulate the expression of other genes. Some appear to play roles in processes we're still characterizing. The story of "junk DNA" has been quietly rewritten over the past two decades as researchers discovered functions in sequences they'd assumed were meaningless.

Maybe the broken GULO gene is just broken. A relic with no current relevance.

Or maybe it does something we haven't found yet.

## What the unknown actually feels like

That uncertainty is what I love most about this. Not knowing isn't a gap — it's an invitation.

There are structures in the genome that we've been quick to dismiss because we couldn't immediately explain them. And then, years later, it turns out they were doing something the whole time — something we didn't have the tools or the framework to see yet.

I think about that a lot when I encounter something in biology that seems to have no clear purpose. Maybe the purpose exists. Maybe we just haven't discovered it yet.

The things we don't understand are not evidence of randomness. They might be evidence of meaning we haven't reached yet. And to me, that is one of the most exciting things about studying this.

> **Disclaimer:** This post is written from a student's perspective for educational purposes. It is not medical advice. Genetic testing decisions should always involve a qualified healthcare professional or genetic counselor.

One of the most common misunderstandings I've encountered — in class discussions, in science journalism, and in casual conversations — is the conflation of **genetic risk** with **genetic diagnosis**. They sound similar. They are fundamentally different.

## What Is a Genetic Diagnosis?

A **genetic diagnosis** identifies a specific pathogenic variant that *causes* a condition. Think of phenylketonuria (PKU): a loss-of-function mutation in the *PAH* gene leads to phenylalanine accumulation, which causes the disorder. If you have the mutation, you have (or will develop) PKU under normal dietary conditions.

Monogenic disorders like PKU, Huntington's disease, or sickle cell anemia follow relatively deterministic rules:
- One gene, one primary mechanism
- High penetrance (having the variant strongly predicts the phenotype)
- Clearer clinical meaning

A genetic test that identifies a *pathogenic* or *likely pathogenic* variant in one of these genes is providing diagnostic information.

## What Is Genetic Risk?

**Genetic risk** describes a *probabilistic* relationship between genotype and phenotype — particularly for polygenic traits and common complex diseases like type 2 diabetes, coronary artery disease, or depression.

**Polygenic risk scores (PRS)** aggregate the effects of thousands of small-effect genetic variants across the genome. An individual with a high PRS for heart disease is statistically more likely (compared to the population average) to develop the condition — but:

- The PRS is calculated from population studies; it doesn't directly predict *your* individual outcome.
- Many people with high PRS never develop the condition.
- Many people with low PRS do develop it (especially with environmental risk factors like smoking, diet, or inactivity).
- PRS accuracy varies significantly across ancestries (most large datasets are still heavily skewed toward European populations).

## The Probability Problem

Here's a way I think about it:

Imagine a PRS that puts you in the top 20% of risk for Type 2 diabetes. That means, among 100 people with your score, roughly X will develop it and Y won't — but you don't know which group you're in. The score shifts probabilities; it doesn't flip a switch.

Compare that to a diagnostic variant in *BRCA1*: pathogenic variants here confer a *lifetime risk* of breast cancer estimated at 50–72% (depending on the variant and study). That's still probabilistic, but the penetrance is high enough that it drives major clinical decisions like increased screening or prophylactic surgery.

## Why Does the Distinction Matter Clinically?

Misinterpreting risk as diagnosis leads to:
- **Over-treatment**: unnecessary procedures, surgeries, or medications for a condition someone may never develop.
- **Under-treatment**: dismissing a high-penetrance variant because "it's just a risk factor."
- **Psychological harm**: anxiety or lifestyle changes based on a misunderstood probability.
- **Missed opportunities**: not using risk information to motivate preventive interventions where they're actually effective.

## Prenatal Context: An Area Requiring Particular Care

Prenatal genetic testing spans a spectrum from screening (probabilistic) to diagnostic:

- **Cell-free fetal DNA (cfDNA) screening** — also called NIPT — screens for chromosomal conditions like trisomy 21. A "positive" result is a high-risk finding, not a diagnosis. False positives occur.
- **Amniocentesis or chorionic villus sampling (CVS)** provide fetal cells for karyotyping or gene sequencing — these are diagnostic, not just screening.

The distinction matters enormously in the clinical conversation that follows a result.

## What I Take Away From This

As a biotechnology student, I find genomic medicine fascinating precisely because of its complexity. The tools are powerful — sequencing costs have collapsed, PRS databases are growing, and newborn screening panels are expanding. But the interpretation layer — the clinical, ethical, and probabilistic reasoning needed to use this information well — is where the real challenge lies.

A test result is not a verdict. It's data. What matters is who interprets it, in what context, and with what support.

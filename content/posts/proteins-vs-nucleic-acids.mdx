One of the first things a biochemistry course hammers into you is the list of biological macromolecules: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids. They're all polymers (or polymer-like) built from simpler monomers — and understanding the monomer-to-polymer logic is the fastest way to retain the details.

This post is my attempt to compare proteins and nucleic acids in a way that sticks — not just memorizing facts, but building a mental model.

## The Monomer Logic

| | Proteins | Nucleic Acids |
|---|---|---|
| **Monomer** | Amino acids (20 standard) | Nucleotides |
| **Polymer** | Polypeptide → folded protein | DNA, RNA |
| **Bond linking monomers** | Peptide bond | Phosphodiester bond |
| **Bond formation** | Condensation (−H₂O) | Condensation (−H₂O) |

Both use condensation (dehydration synthesis) to link monomers — water is released. Both can be broken by hydrolysis — water is added. This parallel is worth noting because it applies across *all* biological polymers.

## Amino Acids: The Building Blocks of Proteins

Twenty standard amino acids differ in their **R groups (side chains)**. The R group determines:
- Polarity (hydrophilic vs. hydrophobic)
- Charge at physiological pH
- Ability to form bonds (e.g., cysteine's disulfide bridges)
- Enzymatic reactivity (e.g., serine as nucleophile in serine proteases)

The sequence of amino acids — the **primary structure** — is encoded in DNA and determines how the protein folds into secondary (α-helix, β-sheet), tertiary, and quaternary structures.

## Nucleotides: The Building Blocks of DNA and RNA

A nucleotide has three components:
1. A **nitrogenous base** (purine or pyrimidine)
2. A **five-carbon sugar** (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA)
3. One or more **phosphate groups**

The bases pair specifically: A–T (in DNA), A–U (in RNA), G–C (in both). This complementarity is what makes replication and transcription possible.

## Functional Roles: Where They Differ Most

**Proteins** are the *workers* of the cell:
- Enzymes (catalysis)
- Structural elements (collagen, actin)
- Signaling molecules (hormones like insulin)
- Transport (hemoglobin)
- Immune defense (antibodies)
- Receptors

**Nucleic acids** are the *archive and messenger*:
- DNA stores genetic information
- mRNA carries the message from DNA to ribosomes
- tRNA adapts the message to amino acids
- rRNA is structural and catalytic in ribosomes

> A useful oversimplification: DNA is the blueprint, RNA is the photocopy sent to the factory floor, and proteins are what the factory actually builds.

## The Central Dogma Connects Them

The central dogma of molecular biology (DNA → RNA → Protein) is the thread that connects these two classes of macromolecule. Information flows from nucleic acid to protein — not in reverse (with some notable exceptions like retroviruses).

Understanding this directionality helps explain why:
- Mutations in DNA can change protein function
- Gene expression can be regulated at transcription (mRNA level) or translation (protein level)
- Diagnostic tests often target either the nucleic acid (PCR) or the protein (ELISA, immunoassay)

## A Note on Blurring Lines

Reality is messier than the textbook:
- **Ribozymes** are RNA molecules with enzymatic activity — so nucleic acids *can* act like proteins.
- **Aptamers** are nucleic acids that bind targets like antibodies.
- Some proteins (like histones) have regulatory roles that feel more "informational."

The categories are useful for organizing knowledge, but they're not hard boundaries.
